## Composition

A comparison makes tradeoffs visible and names a winner. Reach for this surface when
the user's first question is "which of these should I choose, and on what criteria?"
— weighing options, plans, or paths against shared criteria — not when there is a
single recommendation to commit to (that is the [brief](../brief)) or a field of
metrics to resolve into one number (that is the [report](../report)).

**Comparison spread / criteria matrix over cards.** Make tradeoffs visible across
shared criteria like a compact editorial spread — the same rows or columns for every
option, terse parallel labels (Cost, Risk, Timing, Fit, Evidence, Caveat) drawn from
the [mono metadata](../metadata) system, and the winning path anchored by position,
border weight, or an inverse label. Split the options into parallel bands with
`--editorial-column-rule` hairlines at `--editorial-column-gap` so criteria line up
across columns and trust comes from alignment, and rule the criteria rows with
`--editorial-hairline` over the faint `--editorial-ruled-paper` grid. Use the
[ruled evidence bands](../evidence) rhythm to align criteria across options so the
reader compares like against like. Weak or empty cells stay visible; gaps are part
of the comparison. Never use staggered cards, color-coded winners, score bubbles, or
per-option layouts that hide the tradeoff.

**A marked winner, not a colored one.** Mark the chosen option through position,
border weight, or an ink [inverse label](../inverse-panel) rather than decorative
color, and name the accepted tradeoff in one calm sentence. The comparison closes on
the recommended path via the [editorial close](../close), kept shorter and stronger
than the matrix it resolves.
